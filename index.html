Click a theme to follow one of Clark’s arguments across three centuries; click any event for why it matters. Clark’s core claim: nothing about Prussia’s rise was destined, and nothing about its fall was either.

The Prussian story did not happen in a sealed room. Here are the same nine eras with Prussia’s anchor events down the left and the world’s down the right: the wars, revolutions, and ideas the neighbors were living through at the same moment. Click any event for why it matters; gold threads lead back into the Prussian story.

Prussia was never one place. It began as a scatter of unconnected territories with indefensible borders, “a collection of fragments” held together only by the dynasty. Drag the slider to watch it assemble, balloon, collapse, and vanish.

Twelve Hohenzollerns ruled between the Calvinist conversion of 1613 and the abdication of 1918. The dynasty’s habit of alternating between weak and formidable rulers is one of the running jokes of Prussian history.

For most of its life Prussia had no written constitution — it had a balance of forces. Drag the slider and watch the balance shift: circles grow and shrink with each actor’s real weight, arrows say who could compel whom, and the ⚔ marks whose hand was on the army. Click any circle for what its power rested on at that moment.

Why this tab is hereWarum es diesen Teil gibt

A personal note from Kai: Hans-Gert Kirsche (born 1925 in Naumburg) married Margaret Feuerhake, the younger sister of my German grandfather. So the people below are not my ancestors; they are the family of the man who married into ours. He is also not from the aristocratic side of my family tree: the von Bismarcks are my German grandmother’s line, and they now have their own tab, “The Bismarck Thread.” The Kirsches were farmers, the Aschers Jewish shopkeepers in Kiel: the world Clark describes, lived at ground level by people with no castles to lose.

The sourceDie Quelle

Everything in this tab comes from „Herkunft“ (“Origins”), an unpublished memoir of roughly 25,000 words that Hans-Gert wrote around 2003, in his late seventies, from memory, family papers, wartime letters, and the files of the family’s Nazi-era court case and post-war restitution claims. He was a trained philologist and a scrupulous narrator: he flags his own uncertainty, corrects his father’s embellishments, and refuses to dramatize. It is a family document, not a published history — quotations are our translations from his German.

This tab follows my grandmother’s families — the Bismarcks of Briest and the Wendenburgs of Ermsleben — through the whole story the other tabs tell: guild hall, cathedral chapter, ministry, parliament, officer corps, manor house, and the same 1945 as everyone else.

Why this tab is hereWarum es diesen Teil gibt

A personal note from Kai: Irmela von Bismarck is my German grandmother. She turned 100 in July 2022, and the recording she made narrating her birthday slideshow — Berlin, the war, the hospital trains, the flight west — is what started the research behind this page. Her father’s people are the Briest branch of the von Bismarcks, documented back to the Stendal cloth halls of the 1270s and sharing a sixteenth-century ancestor with the chancellor who dominates the later chapters here. Her mother’s people, the Wendenburgs, climbed over two centuries from Meisdorf farmhouses to a Rittergut and a castle, and lost both in a single autumn. Seven centuries inside one state, seen from the manor house and the officer’s mess — ending, like the book itself, in 1945.

The sourcesDie Quellen

Two kinds. The twentieth century comes from Irmela herself: her recorded narration at 100, July 2022 (quotations are our translations, lightly smoothed from the recording). Everything earlier is reconstructed from published genealogies and primary records — Georg Schmidt’s Das Geschlecht von Bismarck (1908), the Gotha and the Genealogisches Handbuch des Adels, the Wendenburg family database built on the 1934 Stammliste, Niekammer’s 1922 estate directory for the Province of Saxony, and the German Wikipedia articles on the line’s public figures. A fuller dossier with source links and open questions lives in this project’s genealogy folder; where sources disagree, the dossier says so, and this tab keeps to what is verified.
