Prussia was never one place. It began as a scatter of unconnected territories with indefensible borders, “a collection of fragments” held together only by the dynasty. Drag the slider to watch it assemble, balloon, collapse, and vanish.

Why this tab is here · Warum es diesen Teil gibt

A personal note from Kai: Hans-Gert Kirsche (born 1925 in Naumburg) married Margaret Feuerhake, the younger sister of my German grandfather. So the people below are not my ancestors; they are the family of the man who married into ours. He is also not from the aristocratic side of my family tree: the von Bismarcks are my German grandmother’s line, and they get their own tab soon. The Kirsches were farmers, the Aschers Jewish shopkeepers in Kiel: the world Clark describes, lived at ground level by people with no castles to lose.

The source · Die Quelle

Everything in this tab comes from „Herkunft“ (“Origins”), an unpublished memoir of roughly 25,000 words that Hans-Gert wrote around 2003, in his late seventies, from memory, family papers, wartime letters, and the files of the family’s Nazi-era court case and post-war restitution claims. He was a trained philologist and a scrupulous narrator: he flags his own uncertainty, corrects his father’s embellishments, and refuses to dramatize. It is a family document, not a published history — quotations are our translations from his German.
